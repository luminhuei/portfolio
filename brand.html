One visual language — from street signage to shipping product.

Overview

A brand rebuilt from scratch became the default visual baseline for every new product.

A full rebranding for a restaurant-tech company — one visual language, running from street signage into the design systems of four product lines.

Constraints: the brand had to serve two completely different kinds of carrier at once — the physical world (signage, uniforms, trade-show materials) and digital products (POS, Kiosk, App, Dashboard).

Key trade‑off: the new standard did not force legacy products to migrate — products with their own design system stayed as they were, while new and eligible products adopted the new system; trading rollout speed for zero migration cost.

Problem

POS, Kiosk, app, and signage each spoke their own language — 700+ stores met a brand with no system to follow.

Approach

One formula — Machine + Interaction + People — as the construction logic, and a mathematical black-white-gray system as the standard: the brand provides order, never steals the show.

Results

The default visual baseline for the company’s new product design — and a 0→1 website sustaining 1.9K–2.4K weekly active users.

Process

01

The brief

700+ stores, zero rules.

Peblla is a restaurant-technology company whose products span POS, Kiosk, handheld devices, ordering apps, and a merchant back office — serving 700+ active stores. Amid rapid growth, the brand layer never had a system to follow: no unified logo rules, no defined color or type system, each product line and each material going its own way. My task when I joined: a full rebranding — a new system from scratch, from brand identity to every product.

02

Who it’s for

Designers first — diners eventually.

The direct users of this brand system are not consumers but the company’s own designers, engineers, and marketing team — the people who have to apply it correctly, day after day, without asking. The end audience is restaurant merchants and diners, who meet the brand again and again on signage, Kiosk screens, and trade-show booths.

03

Strategy & direction

A logo that is a positioning statement.

The brand concept started from a formula: Machine + Interaction + People = Human–Computer Interaction. Peblla’s products are, in essence, the interaction between machines (Kiosk, POS) and people (merchants, diners) — and that positioning became the logo’s construction logic directly: the mark is built from overlapping elements representing machine, interaction, and people. The brand needs no narrative ornament; it is itself a statement of positioning.

For visual direction, I chose engineered restraint: a black-white-gray core, a mathematically defined system of proportions, and no reliance on decorative graphics — because this brand ultimately has to live inside product interfaces, where the real protagonists are the merchant’s data and the diner’s menu. The brand’s job is to provide order, not to steal the show.

04

Logo system

Replacing feeling with math.

The logo is defined by a complete proportional system built on X as the base unit (X / 2X / 4X / 12.5X), governing the mark’s construction, safe spacing, and the icon-to-wordmark relationship. A four-tier size ladder (124 / 64 / 32 / 16px) covers everything from outdoor signage to favicon.

A set of misuse examples spells out “how not to use it” as clearly as “how to use it.”

05

Color

Every shade of gray has a name and a job.

There are only three primary colors: white (background), black (text), and light gray #FAFAFA (background). The real system lives in the grayscale: twelve steps from #FAFAFA to #000000, each annotated with a semantic role — 2% for backgrounds, 1% for strokes, 6% for body text, 100% for headlines.

A separate 12-color functional palette, aligned with iOS system-color conventions, carries state and emphasis. Color isn’t a decorative list; it’s a table of assigned responsibilities.

06

Type, icons & imagery

One family, one scale.

Roboto as a single family in four weights, paired with a complete type scale (Display / Headline / Title / Label / Body, three levels each, with size, line height, and letter-spacing defined). The reason is pragmatic: Roboto renders most reliably on the Android-based restaurant hardware, and the brand font and product font share a single source — no “one font for the brand, another for the product” gap.

A unified icon library with a consistent stroke style, and a fluid-gradient brand image style, give the restrained black-white-gray system a limited emotional outlet.

System in use

Tested not in the proposal — in the real world.

01 Physical World

Building signage, staff uniforms (T-shirts / polos), flyers, a full stationery set, trade-show and outdoor banners — the brand holds up at street scale and at arm’s length.

02 Digital World

The company’s first website — built 0→1 — and the product interfaces themselves, carried by the same grayscale and type scale.

03 Product World

The chain unique to this case: the brand standard became the default baseline for the company’s new product design. Mobile POS’s design system, Report Center’s semantic colors, and Ordering Tablet’s component library all grew out of this grayscale semantics and type-scale system. From brand standard to shipping product interface — one system, one pair of hands.

Impact

The default baseline — by usability, not decree.

After the new brand launched, the standard became the design team’s default working baseline: every designer adopted the new system wherever product conditions allowed; some legacy products with an existing design system stayed as they were — a deliberate trade-off, not a failure of adoption.

The most public piece of the rollout is the official website, peblla.com: post-launch, it steadily sustains roughly 1.9K–2.4K weekly active users — about 90% new visitors, led by direct traffic and organic search. For a B2B restaurant-tech brand, that is a consistent stream of arrivals — and the new brand visual is the first thing every one of them meets.

Reflection

A standard is real when it works in a room with no designer.

The most important trade-off was not forcing legacy products to migrate. Pulling everything up to the new standard would be more “perfect” — but the cost would be several quarters of engineering refactoring, and those products’ users weren’t suffering for the lack of it. We let the system spread on the strength of its own usability: new products adopt by default, eligible products migrate gradually, the rest aren’t forced. Brand consistency is a direction, not a deadline.

It also confirmed something for me: the real output of a brand standard isn’t the document, but whether it gets executed correctly in a room with no designer in it. Semantic grayscale, mathematical logo construction, misuse examples — these all drive the cost of “asking the designer” down to zero. Later, in Mobile POS and Report Center, I built interaction standards into the component layer for the same reason: a good system makes following the rules the most effortless choice.
