A handheld POS that fits in a server’s pocket.

Overview

Within three months of launch, 64% of checkouts moved from the counter to the tableside.

Mobile POS brings ordering, kitchen firing, and checkout to the tableside instead of the counter. It was built around one core question: how do you fit a desktop POS’s core flows onto a single phone screen — and let servers complete tableside work one‑handed, fast, and without errors?

Constraints: a phone screen offers an extremely small operating surface — it cannot carry the desktop POS’s full feature set. Interaction experience benchmarked against Toast GO.

Key trade‑off: cutting features — high‑frequency tableside flows move to the phone and are polished to the extreme, while the rest stays on the countertop POS.

Process

01

The floor problem

One order, three trips.

Walk into a restaurant running a traditional countertop POS and you’ll see the same scene play out: a server jots down the order on paper at the table, walks back to the counter, queues up to enter it into the system, then walks back to the table to confirm. During peak hours these round trips add up, slowing down ordering, kitchen output, and table turnover.

And the moment the network or power goes down, that countertop terminal goes dark — and the entire restaurant grinds to a halt with it. Mobile POS was built to solve exactly these two problems: move ordering and checkout to the tableside, and keep the system reliable even when the network isn’t.

02

Who it’s for

Trained servers, operating by muscle memory.

Mobile POS is built for trained restaurant servers — staff who receive basic onboarding, but who work under real pressure: noise, time constraints, guests watching, standing, device in one hand.

The design goal is therefore not “understandable at first glance” but operable by muscle memory after training, and error-free at peak hours: fixed control positions, shortest paths, and guarded risky actions. This allowed higher information density than a consumer product — but demanded far stricter operational consistency in return.

03

Research

Benchmark, audit, and watch real hands.

We benchmarked Toast GO’s product positioning and interaction patterns — including its approach of surfacing table checks, tabbed by payment status, as a key reference for the order history feature.

We audited the desktop POS’s complete feature list to determine which features had to move to mobile and which could stay behind, and studied how staff actually use devices in real restaurant settings — typically holding the device in the left hand and operating with the right, while standing.

04

Decision one

Feature triage — what deserves a place in the pocket.

A phone screen cannot hold everything the desktop POS does — that is this project’s most fundamental trade-off. We filtered by how often things happen at the table: high-frequency tableside flows — ordering, checkout, order-history management — moved fully onto the phone, one tap away; some low-frequency or complex functions were deliberately left behind on the countertop POS.

Mobile POS was never meant to replace the terminal — it exists to perfect the part worth carrying. The lighter the device in a server’s hand, the faster the service at the table.

05

Decision two

One screen, two audiences.

Mobile POS has a moment a countertop terminal never faces: at checkout, the device turns to face the guest. The audience flips instantly from a trained employee to an untrained customer — one choosing a tip and signing while the server stands right beside them.

So the staff-facing side is optimized for density and speed, while the guest-facing payment and tip screens are deliberately minimal with unmistakable amounts — refined across multiple review rounds to avoid confusion or awkwardness in a socially loaded moment.

06

Decision three

Designing for interruption.

Office software assumes users can finish a flow uninterrupted; a restaurant floor is the opposite — mid-order, another table calls, the kitchen shouts, a guest changes their mind.

Every core flow therefore had to be safely interruptible and resumable: unsubmitted orders hold their state, and servers pick up exactly where they left off instead of starting over.

07

Interaction principles

Rules that run through every screen.

  • Shortest possible operation path — no redundant steps
  • Keyboards auto-invoke wherever input is needed
  • Minimal thumb travel for one-handed use
  • Deliberate friction for irreversible actions — Void and Discount require PIN verification: the one place designed to trade speed for safety
  • Consistent button positions and states across screens

These played out in detail-level refinements: the number pad restyled from outlined keys to solid color blocks, drawers converted to modals for thumb reach, a Confirm vs. Done copy distinction, and a streamlined note-input path — small changes that determine whether a server can operate accurately in a hectic environment.

08

Design system

Decisions locked into components.

With dozens of screens to migrate, designing each page individually would have been slow and impossible to keep consistent. So I built a componentized design system:

  • Component variants for buttons, inputs, modals, and list items — covering size, state (default / pressed / disabled), and use case
  • Semantic color definitions so Light and Dark Mode share one component structure — no parallel design files to maintain
  • Interaction rules baked into components — any new screen inherits the standards automatically

Screen design turned from “drawing page by page” into “assembling from components,” and design and engineering gained a single source of truth — handoff costs dropped dramatically.

Solution

Following a server’s complete service journey.

01 Seating & Table Management

Servers switch freely between Table View — mirroring the restaurant’s actual floor layout — and List View, built for quickly locating orders. Table states are color-coded: open, dining, and overtime tables each carry a dedicated status color, so a server reads the entire floor in a single glance — where to seat, who is dining, which table has run long and needs attention for turnover — no reading table by table. In a noisy, time-pressed environment, color is a faster information channel than text: the same judgment behind the color-block number pad. Once a table is assigned, every subsequent action belongs to that table.

02 Tableside Ordering & Modifiers

The moment the guest finishes speaking, the order is already in the kitchen. For menus that routinely run to hundreds of items, every dish is color-coded by category — seafood, drinks, and house specials each carry a dedicated color: servers lock onto the color zone first, then read the name to confirm, turning “finding the dish” in a dense button grid from item-by-item reading into visual scanning. Add-ons, allergies, and special requests are captured on the spot — selecting an item leads straight into the input view with the keyboard auto-invoked.

03 Coursing & Delay Send

Guests want appetizers first and ramen after — but ramen comes out faster. Servers set a Delay Send on specific items, so the kitchen fires dishes on the guest’s rhythm, not the kitchen’s.

04 KDS Integration

Orders sent from the tableside appear instantly on kitchen screens, and preparation status flows back to the handheld — no more verbal relay or paper tickets between front and back of house.

05 Split Check & Flexible Payments

Groups split the check for individual payment; contactless cards, digital wallets, gift cards, and loyalty points are all accepted at the table. Tip selection and payment complete in one uninterrupted flow.

06 Plate Scanning Checkout

For conveyor-belt sushi, every plate carries a scannable identifier. A server points Mobile POS at a stack of plates, and the system instantly tallies the table’s consumption and completes checkout — replacing manual plate counting entirely.

+ Always dependable

Beyond the main journey: Order History organizes orders into Open / Authed / Closed tabs with edit, payment, refund, and reprint actions; Light / Dark Mode adapts to bright lunch windows and dim evening bars, sharing one component set through semantic colors; and offline reliability keeps core ordering running through network instability — even a full POS outage — syncing automatically on recovery.

Impact

Checkout left the counter — and stayed gone.

After launch, the most immediate change happened at checkout: within three months, 64% of checkouts shifted from the countertop POS to tableside Mobile POS. Servers stopped shuttling back and forth for payment, and the guest’s wait from asking for the check to walking out the door was substantially compressed.

Industry research supports the direction: handheld POS paired with well-designed workflows typically delivers a 15–35% improvement in table-turn efficiency — and interface design is the precondition for all of it. If a server fumbles at the table, guests will simply ask to pay at the counter.

Reflection

Principles before pixels — and error-proof beats beautiful.

Moving a desktop system to a phone, the biggest trap is shrinking page by page instead of redesigning. With principles locked in first — one-handed operability, minimal thumb travel, added friction for risky actions — dozens of screen migrations became “apply the principle” rather than “argue it again.”

And in high-pressure environments, error-proof matters far more than beautiful: changes that looked minor in design reviews — the color-block number pad, drawers-to-modals, Confirm vs. Done — determined whether frontline staff could operate by muscle memory at peak hours.

If I did this again, I’d push earlier for small-scale field observation in real restaurants — issues like readability in direct sunlight and mis-taps from greasy fingers only reveal themselves in the real world.
