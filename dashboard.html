Turning numbers into answers.

Overview

The daily business window for operators of 700+ active stores.

The Analytics Dashboard (Report Center) is a business data hub for restaurant owners — letting operators with no data background understand today’s business in seconds, trace every dollar when they need to, and trust every number on the screen.

Constraints: the specification and accounting definitions were owned by the PM and benchmarked to industry standards — not to be deviated from. One web app had to serve both desktop and mobile reading postures.

Key trade‑off: we invested more engineering resources and polish time in UI readability than a typical reporting build — trading higher development cost for reports owners can read simply and clearly.

Process

01

The scattered answer

One question, four places to look.

Restaurant owners answer the same question every day: how is my business actually doing? But the answer is scattered everywhere — sales data lives in the POS, delivery data on third-party platforms, deposits on bank statements, tips and refunds in yet other reports. Nobody has time to piece these fragments into a conclusion they can act on.

The core question: how do you let an owner with no data background understand today’s business in seconds, trace every dollar when needed — and trust every number on the screen?

02

Who it’s for

They don’t want data. They want answers.

The primary users are restaurant owners and franchisees — not data analysts, with zero report training. Their days are consumed by running the store; the time they have for data might be five minutes before opening, or a gap after the rush.

What they want isn’t data — it’s answers: Is today better than yesterday? Did all the money come in? Which channel is growing?

03

The spec

A mature category — where presentation decides.

Reporting follows well-established standards in restaurant SaaS, so the specification came from two lines: industry conventions — a dedicated PM owned the reporting logic, benchmarked against Toast’s Settlement Time business-day definition, 3rd Party Excl./Incl. accounting toggles, and per-transaction fee breakdown granularity — and the real needs and business planning of restaurant owners: the operating questions they actually use these numbers to answer. Together they formed a clear, complete specification.

My work started from that spec: turning industry-standard information logic into an interface an owner can read at a glance. The spec guarantees every page is correct; the UI decides whether an owner “gets it in seconds” or drowns in it — three principles carried that work across the whole reporting family.

04

Principle one

Turning numbers into answers.

An owner’s real question is never “how much did we sell today” — it’s “is this number good?” Is $21,862 something to celebrate or worry about? Without a reference point, a number is just a number.

So Sales Overview’s core metrics default to a comparison against yesterday, with green-up / red-down percentages sitting right beside the big numbers — no mental math, no remembering yesterday’s figures. Net Sales, Payments, and Refunds anchor the page as three top cards, while breakdowns by payment method, channel, and dining type fold into tabs inside each card: visual weight strictly follows how often owners ask the question, not how complex the data is.

05

Principle two

Data honesty as interface language.

A dashboard’s biggest risk isn’t looking bad — it’s an owner discovering the numbers don’t reconcile, and never trusting it again. The spec includes many mechanisms protecting reconciliation accuracy; the UI’s job is to make them visible, understandable, and unthreatening:

  • Honest disclosure on third-party data: fees and refunds can cause mismatches — the interface says so directly and offers a 3rd Party Excl./Incl. toggle, instead of pretending the data is perfect
  • Sales vs Payment reconciliation & Unclosed Orders: the gap between billed and collected sits right on the surface, making differences explainable
  • Data sync status: when data isn’t real-time, the interface states the cutoff clearly

These notices consistently use informational yellow rather than error-level red — informing without alarming is the discipline of designing for trust.

06

Principle three

One web app, two reading postures.

Desktop and mobile browsers log into the same system, but the two devices serve entirely different postures. Desktop carries “sitting down to reconcile”: full reports, line-item tables, download and export. Mobile re-composes everything into a vertical card stream for “checking the pulse” — an owner pulls out their phone in any spare moment and knows how the business is doing within 30 seconds.

Same data: desktop reads it in tables, mobile scans it in cards.

07

Design system

One language for the whole reporting family.

I joined when Report Center was just getting started — the first wave covered Sales Overview, Sales Summary, Order / Transaction Details, and the Weekly Report, so the whole family grew under one design language from its very first pages. It isn’t one page but a family of reports living on two breakpoints, and my most important output was the design system built to carry it:

  • Metric card component: large value + period-over-period indicator, with globally unified semantic colors (green up, red down) — new metrics plug straight in
  • Chart and table standards: unified styles and interactions across bar charts, trend charts, and line-item tables
  • Tiered notice banners: disclaimers and sync status componentized, expressed uniformly across pages
  • Responsive re-composition rules: each component’s desktop-to-mobile reflow is componentized — new report pages support both devices by default

Adding a new report page means assembling components, not designing from scratch. The spec guarantees every page is correct; the design system guarantees every page speaks the same language — learn to read one report, and you’ve learned to read them all.

Solution

Four modules, to the rhythm of an owner’s questions.

01 Sales Overview — how is business today

Net Sales, Payments, and Refunds as three core cards with period-over-period indicators set the tone at a glance, with breakdowns folded into tabs inside each card. The Sales vs Payment reconciliation area and Unclosed Orders make differences explainable, and the Miscellaneous and Performance blocks round out taxes, tips, auto gratuity, and voided amounts — every restaurant-specific line item accounted for.

02 Sales Summary — how is the rhythm

Sales by Day, Day of Week, and Time of Day charts reveal the rhythm; summary cards below — Net Sales, Revenue, Cash, Payment, Server Sales, Unclosed Orders — cover the books. Date presets pair with a dual-calendar custom range, and a “day” means the business day, not the calendar day — stores closing after midnight settle at 5 AM, with Settlement Time configurable.

03 Order / Transaction Details — where every dollar went

Every order’s full fee chain laid out transparently — amount, tips, auto gratuity, delivery fee, Dasher tips, transaction and additional fees, down to the final Net Total, with prepaid amounts and refunds accounted for — plus Payment / Refund / Adjust Fee categorization and export. Each transaction carries its business dimensions too, from dining type and channel to split payment and promotions; when data isn’t real-time, the page states the cutoff clearly.

04 Weekly Report — the week in review

Ten modules review the whole operation: Net Sales, Upsells, Third Party, Direct Delivery, Top Sales Items, channel distribution, sales & upsell trends, member analysis, Google / Yelp review sentiment, and off-premise geo distribution. The entire report is designed for mobile reading as a vertical card stream — where Toast sends an email digest, Peblla made it a complete report readable in one scroll on the phone.

Impact

The value of a report is quiet.

Report Center is the business window Peblla merchants open every day, serving the operators of over 700 active stores — on a platform processing roughly $27.98M in monthly GMV across 920K orders.

A reporting product’s success shows in two quiet things: whether owners build the habit of checking daily, and whether calls to their Account Manager asking “the numbers don’t match” decline. A report that doesn’t read as a judgment turns every confusion into a phone call; one with clear hierarchy and consistent semantics absorbs those calls into the interface — report design quality shows up directly in the cost of human support.

Reflection

In a mature category, the designer’s value is the precision of presentation.

How do you measure the success of a report? Every store looks at its reports — “being used” is guaranteed, but it isn’t the same as “well designed.”

If I did this again, I’d instrument two metrics from day one: the frequency of Account Manager inquiries, and whether owners check in proactively every day. Where the specification follows industry consensus, a UI designer’s value isn’t inventing new logic — it’s the precision of presentation.
